The Memberstack vs Outseta question comes up on almost every gated-content project I scope, and the answer is usually settled in the first ten minutes — once you've answered whether the site needs billing, how many members you expect, and whether you want to replace your CRM and email tool at the same time. The short version: Webflow Memberships for free or manually managed gating under 500 users, Memberstack for paid subscriptions and clean user auth, Outseta when you want one platform to replace three tools. Everything else is implementation details.

I've shipped gated sites across all three platforms in the last two years. The one I reach for changes with the brief, not with personal loyalty. If you're already scoping a project and want a second opinion before committing to a stack, [get in touch](/contact) — this is exactly the conversation I find most useful to have early.

## One-line verdict per use case

Five scenarios, one recommendation each. If your situation maps cleanly to one of these, you don't need to read further.

| Use case | Pick | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Free members-only content, small list | Webflow Memberships | Native, zero extra cost, no third-party dependency |
| Paid subscription site with tiers | Memberstack | Stripe billing built in, tier upgrades handled natively |
| SaaS product with user accounts | Memberstack or Outseta | Memberstack for B2C, Outseta if you need CRM alongside |
| Membership site replacing HubSpot + Mailchimp | Outseta | Bundles email, CRM, and billing in one platform |
| Online course with gated lessons | Memberstack | Webflow-native UX, per-content access control, tested at scale |

For more on the course use case specifically, the [webflow-gated-course](/blog/webflow-gated-course) post walks through a full implementation.

## Webflow Memberships: strengths and ceilings

Webflow Memberships is genuinely good at one thing: gating content for a small, manually managed user base where billing is handled outside the platform or not at all. I covered this in full in the [Webflow Memberships review](/blog/webflow-memberships-review), but the relevant points for a comparison are these.

The strengths are real. The login and signup pages are native Webflow Designer pages, not external redirects — you style them like any other page and they match your brand without a line of custom CSS. CMS item-level gating is cleaner than anything Memberstack or Outseta offers natively: you can gate individual collection items, not just pages, which matters for gated resource libraries and partial content archives. And because it's a Webflow-native feature, there's no JavaScript snippet loading time between page load and authentication check — session state is available immediately.

The ceilings show up fast once you move past simple gating. There is no native billing. Seriously — Webflow Memberships has no Stripe integration, no subscription management, no trial logic, and no dunning. If you want to charge for access, you're bolting Stripe Checkout on manually via webhooks, which works but adds three or four failure modes you're now responsible for monitoring. User profile fields are limited to name and email unless you layer Webflow Logic or a third-party API on top. Access groups cap at ten, which is fine for most sites but creates headaches on complex tiered products. And the feature set has been essentially stable for over a year — no new billing capabilities are on the public roadmap.

The right mental model for Webflow Memberships: it's an access control layer, not a membership platform. When the brief calls for an access control layer and nothing more, it's the cleanest tool in the stack.

## Memberstack: the full-stack user layer

Memberstack is what Webflow Memberships would be if Webflow had decided to build a fully realized membership product instead of a gating feature. The integration model is similar — a JS snippet in your Webflow project's custom code section — but the feature set is an order of magnitude deeper.

The native Stripe integration is the headline. You configure plans in Memberstack's dashboard, connect your Stripe account, and Memberstack handles checkout, subscription management, trial periods, upgrade and downgrade flows, failed payment retry, and dunning without a single line of custom webhook code. The user goes through a Memberstack-hosted checkout modal (which can be styled to match your site fairly closely) and lands in the correct access tier immediately. No Cloudflare Worker, no Zapier, no serverless function catching Stripe events at 2am.

Access control works differently from Webflow Memberships. Memberstack uses data attributes on Webflow elements — `ms-hide` and `ms-show` attributes on any HTML element, and page-level access restrictions configured in the Memberstack dashboard. The UX is Webflow-native: users see your Webflow site, not an external auth page. Login and signup modals are injected into your existing pages via data attributes. The result is a gating experience that feels like it was built into Webflow, even though it's running through a JS layer.

Memberstack 2.0 added team accounts and seat-based billing, which changed the B2B calculus considerably. Before 2.0, Memberstack was clearly a B2C tool. Now it handles use cases like agency client portals, small SaaS products with per-seat pricing, and internal tools with shared team access. It's not Auth0 — the enterprise identity management depth isn't there — but for a B2B product with under 200 customer accounts, it's a workable and much cheaper alternative.

The friction points worth knowing: Memberstack's per-member cost adds up past 1,000 members in a way that Outseta's flat pricing doesn't. The JS snippet approach, while clean, does add a small page load dependency — if Memberstack's CDN hiccups, your auth layer is affected. And the member dashboard UI, while customizable, is more opinionated than rolling your own with Webflow CMS dynamic pages. These are manageable tradeoffs, not dealbreakers, but they're worth knowing before you scope a 50,000-member site on Memberstack's starter tier.

For the course use case specifically, Memberstack's per-content access control pairs extremely well with Webflow's CMS. You can lock individual CMS collection items to specific Memberstack plans, build a native-looking course progress experience in Webflow, and handle all billing through Memberstack's Stripe integration. I've deployed this pattern and it holds up well.

## Outseta: CRM + subscriptions + members

Outseta's product positioning is fundamentally different from the other two. Memberstack and Webflow Memberships are membership layers you add to your existing stack. Outseta is a replacement for your stack — specifically, for the combination of a CRM, an email marketing tool, a help desk, and a membership/billing platform.

The core offering bundles subscription billing (via Stripe), a lightweight CRM, email marketing with basic automation, a help desk / live chat widget, and membership access control, all under one platform and one monthly fee. For a small SaaS or membership business that would otherwise be paying for HubSpot Starter, Mailchimp, Intercom, and Memberstack separately, the consolidation can remove significant tool overhead. The [Webflow HubSpot integration](/blog/webflow-hubspot-integration) post gives a sense of how much plumbing a CRM integration normally requires — Outseta removes that entire category of work.

The Webflow integration is solid but slightly heavier than Memberstack. Outseta's login and checkout flows use an iframe or overlay widget that loads Outseta's hosted UI into your Webflow site. The UX is reasonably clean, but it's visually distinct from a Memberstack modal that can be styled to match your Webflow design system. If brand consistency on the auth flow is a priority, Memberstack wins narrowly. If consolidating four tools into one is the priority, Outseta's slightly heavier auth UX is an easy tradeoff.

Outseta's CRM is lightweight — think a structured contact database with pipeline stages, not Salesforce. For a small SaaS founder who is also their own sales rep, it's adequate. For a team running structured outbound or complex deal flows, it'll feel limiting. The email marketing is similarly capable for basic sequences and broadcasts, but not in the same tier as ActiveCampaign or even Mailchimp for complex automation.

Where Outseta shines is the all-in-one user object. Every Outseta contact is also a billing entity, a member, a CRM record, and an email subscriber. When someone upgrades their plan, their CRM record reflects it, their email tag updates, and their access level changes — in one action, in one platform. Keeping those systems in sync manually, or through Zapier, is the tax you pay with any non-Outseta stack. For a solo founder or a small team, eliminating that tax is worth more than the feature depth Memberstack or HubSpot has in individual modules.

## Pricing across the three

What you actually pay depends heavily on user volume and which features you need. Here's the baseline picture:

| Platform | Starting price | Free tier | Per-user cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Webflow Memberships | $0 (included on $39/mo Business plan) | No free tier separate from site plan | No per-user fee |
| Memberstack | $25/mo (Starter) | No free tier | $0.00 per member on Starter up to 500; tiered above |
| Outseta | $39/mo (Founder) | No free tier | No per-member fee; priced by plan tier |

The Webflow Memberships cost picture is deceptive. The feature itself is free on the Business plan, but Business costs $39/month for site hosting. If you're already on Business, Memberships costs nothing additional. If you're on a cheaper Webflow plan, you're effectively paying $39/mo to unlock it.

Memberstack's per-member pricing is the critical variable for high-volume sites. On the Starter plan ($25/mo), you get up to 500 members. Past that, you move up tiers — the cost per member decreases at scale, but for a site with 5,000 active paying members, you're on a plan costing $149–$299/month depending on features needed. That's still reasonable for a paid membership business generating revenue, but it's not a "set it and forget it" number.

Outseta's flat-tier model is the most predictable. The Founder plan at $39/mo includes all features with limits on contacts and emails per month, and scales to a Startup plan at $79/mo and a Growth plan at $159/mo. No per-member surcharge. If you're running a large free membership with thousands of users and a smaller paid tier, Outseta's pricing structure tends to work out better than Memberstack's.

The honest cost comparison requires knowing your projected member volume and whether you're currently paying separately for CRM, email, and billing tools. For many bootstrapped SaaS or membership businesses, the TCO of Outseta is lower than the combination of Mailchimp + HubSpot Starter + Memberstack even though Outseta's sticker price looks similar.

## Dev lift to integrate

All three tools have different integration profiles. The "easy to get started" experience doesn't always reflect the total dev time for a production-ready implementation.

**Webflow Memberships:** lowest initial lift, highest operational complexity if you add billing. Getting gating live is two hours of work — enable in site settings, create access groups, restrict pages. Adding billing through the Stripe + webhook approach takes a full day minimum and requires writing and hosting a serverless function, testing webhook delivery end to end, and building a failure-recovery flow for users who paid but didn't get access. Every additional billing state (cancellation, trial expiry, payment failure) is another webhook handler to write and test.

**Memberstack:** one day of setup, clean ongoing maintenance. Drop the JS snippet, connect Stripe, configure plans, add data attributes to Webflow elements, test checkout flows. The initial setup is slightly more work than Webflow Memberships' free gating, but you're done with the billing integration in the same session — no separate serverless infrastructure to manage. Ongoing changes to plans, prices, or access rules are made in the Memberstack dashboard, not in code. Team account setup adds half a day if needed.

**Outseta:** one to two days of setup, less ongoing complexity if you were planning to build multiple tools anyway. The longest part is configuring email sequences, setting up CRM pipeline stages, and customizing the widget styling to match your Webflow design. If you're coming from a clean-slate project (no existing HubSpot or Mailchimp data), setup is straightforward. If you're migrating contacts from existing tools, budget time for data cleanup.

<Callout type="warning">
All three tools create platform dependency on your user auth and membership data. Migrating members from Memberstack to Outseta (or vice versa) mid-project requires exporting member records, reconciling billing states in Stripe, and rebuilding access rules — typically a multi-day dev engagement. Choose your platform before you accumulate meaningful member volume, not after. The integration decision is harder to reverse than any front-end choice you make on the Webflow side.
</Callout>

## The decision matrix

If the one-line verdicts in section one didn't land a clear answer, this matrix should close it.

| Requirement | Webflow Memberships | Memberstack | Outseta |
|---|---|---|---|
| Free content gating | Best fit | Works, adds cost | Works, adds cost |
| Paid subscriptions (Stripe) | Build yourself | Native | Native |
| Tiered pricing (3+ tiers) | Painful to maintain | Native | Native |
| B2B team accounts | Not supported | Memberstack 2.0 | Native |
| CRM built in | No | No | Yes |
| Email marketing built in | No | No | Yes |
| Help desk / live chat | No | No | Yes |
| Webflow UX fidelity | Best (native) | Excellent | Good (iframe overlay) |
| Per-member pricing | None | Yes (after 500) | None |
| Best for solo/small teams | Yes (free) | Yes (paid) | Yes (consolidated) |
| Complex billing logic | No | Yes | Yes |
| Migration risk | Low | Medium | Medium |

<Callout type="tip">
There's a hybrid pattern that works well for content-heavy sites that want to keep free members on Webflow Memberships while running paid tiers through Memberstack. Keep your free access group in Webflow Memberships — it costs nothing and is native. For paid tiers, use Memberstack exclusively. The two platforms can coexist on the same Webflow site because Memberstack adds its auth layer via JS without touching the Webflow Memberships configuration. You avoid Memberstack's per-member cost for free users while getting its full billing suite for the tiers that generate revenue. The implementation takes about a day to wire correctly, but the ongoing economics are favorable for sites with large free audiences and smaller paid cohorts.
</Callout>

The real deciding question is whether you're building a membership product or a gated content layer. A gated content layer needs access control and nothing else — that's Webflow Memberships, and the native integration justifies it. A membership product needs billing, user profiles, onboarding flows, and subscription management — that's Memberstack, unless you also need a CRM and email tool, in which case it's Outseta. The tools are different enough that choosing the wrong one at the start isn't a styling decision you can fix later. It's a rebuild. If you're unsure which side of that line your project sits on, [get in touch](/contact) — scoping the membership requirements before choosing the platform is exactly the kind of problem where an early conversation saves a significant amount of later engineering time.
