White-label Webflow development is simple in concept: a design agency hires a white label webflow developer to build sites under the agency's brand, the client never knows a third party was involved, and the agency marks up the work at a healthy margin. In practice, the arrangement works beautifully when both sides understand what they're actually agreeing to — and falls apart quickly when they don't.

I've been the developer in these partnerships for a number of agencies across the US and Europe. The ones that run well have a few things in common: complete design files, a single agency-side point of contact, and clear agreement upfront on who does what. The ones that fail almost always have the same root cause, which I'll get to at the end.

If you're trying to decide whether a freelance developer or a white-label agency arrangement fits your situation, [Webflow freelancer vs agency](/blog/webflow-freelancer-vs-agency) covers the tradeoffs in detail. If you're an agency already thinking about how to structure this, [get in touch](/contact) and I can walk you through how I run these engagements.

## What white-label Webflow actually means

White-label means the deliverable is unbranded from my perspective and fully branded from yours. I build a site in your Webflow workspace (or a workspace you'll transfer to the client), I communicate only with your team, and if the client ever asks who built their site, the answer is your agency. My name goes nowhere.

The agreement has three practical layers. First, I do the Webflow work: build, QA, responsive testing, and CMS setup. Second, I keep the relationship invisible — no direct client contact unless you specifically authorize it, no mentions of my involvement in delivered files or code comments, no portfolio credit without your written sign-off. Third, you handle everything client-facing: discovery, design, project management, billing, and launch.

This is a clean division when it holds. Where agencies sometimes blur the lines — asking me to join a client call "just to answer technical questions," or requesting I weigh in on design decisions — the arrangement gets complicated. I'm not the client's developer, I'm yours. Every client touchpoint that passes through me instead of you creates risk for both parties.

The work itself looks like any [Webflow development services](/blog/webflow-development-services) engagement: responsive build from a design file, CMS schema and content entry, interaction setup, SEO technical foundation, and QA across browsers and viewports. The difference is entirely in the workflow, not the output.

My [recent work](/work) can give you a sense of what the builds actually look like, even though most white-label projects aren't in my public portfolio by definition.

## Scope: what gets outsourced, what stays in-house

The cleanest white-label partnerships have a sharp line. Your agency keeps: client discovery, brand strategy, UX and visual design, content direction, copywriting, launch approvals, and the ongoing client relationship. I handle: Webflow build, responsive implementation, CMS architecture, interaction coding, accessibility basics, performance QA, and staging setup.

When scope bleeds across that line, it gets expensive. The most common bleed is design. An agency sends me a brief and a few rough wireframes expecting me to interpret the design intent and fill in the gaps. That's not a Webflow build — that's a design-plus-build engagement at a higher price point. If your Figma file isn't final when we start, either delay the project until it is, or explicitly add design as a billable line item.

The second common bleed is content. Agencies sometimes bundle copywriting into their client quote without realizing they haven't arranged for anyone to actually write the copy. I can populate CMS items with placeholder content to demonstrate the structure, but if real copy isn't delivered before we need it in the build, the project stalls. Content delivery is the agency's responsibility to coordinate.

What I'll proactively own: pointing out when a Figma component won't work the way it was drawn in Webflow (and proposing a workable alternative), flagging CMS structure decisions that'll cause pain later, and raising SEO or performance issues I notice during the build. Those are things a good developer catches on your behalf so you don't have to explain them to the client after launch.

## Pricing models: cost-plus, fixed, retainer

How you pay me and how you bill the client are two separate decisions, but they usually run in parallel.

| Model | When it fits | Typical rate range |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed-price per project | Scope is fully defined before kickoff — page count, design file complete, integrations listed | $3,000–$12,000/project |
| Hourly cost-plus | Scope has ambiguity or the agency wants to pass time through to the client with a markup | $45–$90/hr |
| Monthly retainer | Agency has recurring Webflow work across multiple clients, needs capacity on standby | $2,500–$6,000/month |

Fixed-price is what most agencies prefer because it makes client budgeting clean. I'll do fixed-price when the design is finished and the scope is written. "We'll finalize the pages as we go" is not a scope I'll put a fixed price on — it becomes an hourly engagement by default.

Cost-plus works when the agency's contract with the client is also time-and-materials. I invoice the agency at my rate; the agency adds their margin and invoices the client. This is transparent and easy to administer. It requires the agency to trust that my hours are accurate, which is why I log everything and provide weekly summaries.

Retainer arrangements suit agencies that have consistent Webflow volume — say, two to three client projects per month at varying scales. A monthly retainer reserves a block of my time for your pipeline. You get predictable capacity; I get predictable revenue. For most single-project white-label arrangements, a retainer isn't necessary. For agencies doing volume, it's worth considering. I covered the tradeoffs in [Webflow retainer vs project](/blog/webflow-retainer-vs-project) if you want the full breakdown.

One pricing note: agencies typically mark up white-label developer costs 2–3× to the client. A $5,000 build from me gets billed at $10,000–$15,000. That's standard, that's healthy, and I have no issue with it. The markup pays for your account management, design work, QA oversight, client communication, and the risk you're carrying on the relationship. You're not just reselling my time — you're delivering a complete service.

## NDA, invoicing, and who the client sees

The legal layer doesn't need to be complicated. A two-page mutual NDA covers the important ground: I don't disclose that I worked on the project, I don't contact the client directly, and neither of us poaches the other's clients. Add a no-poach clause explicitly — "Agency won't hire Developer directly without 12 months' written consent" protects me; "Developer won't approach Agency's clients directly" protects you.

I invoice the agency, not the client. My invoices reference the project name or an internal code that means nothing to anyone outside our relationship. The client never sees my name on any financial document.

Project ownership sits in your Webflow workspace. I work inside your workspace from day one, which means when the project is done, you control the transfer. If a Webflow account transfer to the client is part of the deal, you handle that — I don't have a direct relationship with the client's billing or hosting setup.

One practical note on communication: all project messages should go through a single channel (Slack, email, Linear, whatever your agency uses) with your project manager in the thread. I don't need a separate direct line to the client's stakeholders, and in most cases it's better for everyone if that line doesn't exist. When clients start talking directly to the developer, the agency's context gets bypassed, scope requests come in out of order, and the project manager loses visibility. Keep the communication structure clean.

## Design handoff spec I ask every agency for

Before I start building, I need a specific set of assets and decisions from your design team. This is the handoff spec I send every new agency partner at project kickoff.

<Callout type="tip">
Every Figma file must include a shared style library with text styles, color styles, and component variants explicitly named — not embedded in frames or implied by the designs. If your designer named an element "text/body/regular" in Figma, I can match it to a Webflow class. If they used an unnamed text layer at 16px Graphik in three different places without a style, I have to interpret intent, which costs time and introduces inconsistency.
</Callout>

The full spec I ask for:

**Figma file requirements:** All pages designed at 1440px desktop and 375px mobile at minimum. Typography styles named and using the brand typeface (not Figma system fonts). Color styles named to match the brand palette. Components built as Figma components (not copied frames). Any interactive states — hover, active, open/closed — designed, not described in a comment.

**Breakpoint decisions:** Tell me upfront whether you want tablet (991px or 768px) designed, or whether I'm interpreting it. For most marketing sites I can handle tablet responsively from desktop and mobile designs, but if the design has complex tablet-specific layouts, I need those frames.

**Animations:** Any scroll-triggered animations, hover interactions, or page transitions should be annotated in Figma with a brief description of the behavior. "Fade up on scroll" is enough. "As described in the deck" is not.

**CMS content:** A content map — even a spreadsheet — listing what types of dynamic content exist, how many items to expect in year one, and what fields are needed. This determines the CMS schema before I build a single collection.

**Assets:** SVG logos, any icon sets, font files or typeface names. If you're using a licensed typeface that isn't on Google Fonts, I need the font files or a licensed CDN link, not a reminder to "source them."

This is covered in more detail in [Figma to Webflow handoff](/blog/figma-to-webflow-handoff), which I wrote specifically for design teams handing off to a Webflow developer.

## Build pace: what a single dev ships per week

Agencies sometimes underestimate how fast a focused Webflow developer can move on a well-defined project — and sometimes overestimate it when the scope isn't tight. Here's what a realistic week-by-week schedule looks like for a 10-page marketing site with a CMS blog.

| Week | Typical deliverable |
|---|---|
| Week 1 | Site architecture, global styles, design system (typography, colors, spacing, components), homepage build |
| Week 2 | Inner pages (about, services, contact), responsive pass on all built pages, CMS schema finalized |
| Week 3 | Blog templates, CMS content population with real or placeholder content, integrations (forms, analytics, embeds) |
| Week 4 | Full QA pass — cross-browser, mobile, Lighthouse performance audit, accessibility checks, client review staging link |

That's an aggressive but achievable pace for a project where the design is done, assets are delivered, and content is available on schedule. Add a week for each of these: design revisions that come in mid-build, missing content at the start of week three, or integrations with third-party tools that weren't scoped upfront.

For CMS-heavy sites — a blog with 100+ posts, a case study library, a team directory — add one to two weeks for content architecture and data migration. Importing content into Webflow CMS from another platform is manageable but takes careful field mapping and QA on every content type.

On any white-label engagement, I send a progress summary every Friday: what's done, what's in progress, any blockers, and what I need from the agency side by Monday. That's enough for your project manager to status-update the client without passing anything through me.

## QA and revision cycles

QA isn't a phase that happens at the end — it runs throughout the build. But the formal QA pass before handing off to the agency for review is the most important gate in the process.

My standard QA checklist covers: responsive behavior at 1440px, 1280px, 1024px, 768px, 375px; Chrome, Safari, and Firefox on desktop; Safari on iOS and Chrome on Android; Lighthouse performance (targeting 85+ on mobile, 90+ on desktop); all forms tested for submission and routing; all CMS-filtered lists checked with edge-case content lengths; all interactions at reduced motion preference; meta titles and descriptions populated; no broken links on any page.

I deliver QA to the agency as a password-protected staging link on the Webflow subdomain or your whitelisted custom domain. You review it, collect feedback internally from your team and any client stakeholders, and send me a single consolidated revision list. I don't work from fragmented feedback — ten Slack messages sent at different times about different pages is not a revision list. One document, one round.

Standard white-label engagements include two revision rounds in the project price. The first is for significant changes — things that came out wrong or that the design didn't anticipate. The second is for final polish. Changes beyond that scope (adding pages, changing the CMS structure, redesigning sections) are change orders billed hourly.

This revision structure protects the agency too. When there's a defined process, the client's endless refinement cycle doesn't become your headache to absorb. "We get two revision rounds" is a policy you can communicate clearly because you know I'll hold that line.

## When white-label doesn't work

This is the section that saves everyone time if they read it before starting.

<Callout type="warning">
The single most common failure mode: the agency brings in a white-label developer before the design is finished, expecting the developer to push the project forward while design is still in progress. The developer starts building assumptions, the design changes, built sections need to be rebuilt, and the project costs twice what it should have while nobody is officially responsible for the overrun.
</Callout>

White-label doesn't work when the agency hasn't done design yet. If you're at the "we have a rough direction and some reference sites" stage, you're not ready for a Webflow developer. Get your Figma files finished first. The developer's job is to build a design accurately — not to invent the design, make design decisions under time pressure, or fill in gaps your designer didn't resolve. When you're actually ready, a Webflow developer can move fast. Pulling one in prematurely slows everything down.

It doesn't work when the client expects to meet "the team" that built their site. Some clients ask specific questions about who coded their site, want to have a technical conversation with the developer, or expect the developer to be on an ongoing support call. If your client relationship is structured that way, a white-label arrangement puts you in a difficult spot. Either manage the client's expectation upfront ("our build team is internal and not client-facing") or hire someone you can introduce openly.

It doesn't work when the agency can't give specific feedback. The review process I described above assumes your team can look at a built page and say "the spacing above this heading should be 48px not 64px" or "this card needs the hover state from the Figma component." Vague feedback — "it doesn't feel quite right" or "the client wants it more modern" — isn't actionable. I can't fix a feeling. If your team doesn't have the vocabulary to give technical design feedback, the revision cycles will be long and frustrating for both sides.

It doesn't work when the agency is using the white-label arrangement as a way to skip project management. Having a developer takes care of the build, but someone on your side still needs to own the timeline, coordinate content, consolidate feedback, and make decisions when there are competing opinions from the client stakeholders. White-label replaces your Webflow execution, not your project management.

White-label works when the agency delivers complete design and owns the client. It breaks when either half is missing.
