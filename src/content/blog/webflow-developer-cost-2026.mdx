Webflow developer cost in 2026 runs from about $1,500 for a lean landing page to $28,000+ for a full SaaS marketing site with a blog, integrations, and complex animations. The number you should care about is not the hourly rate — it's what you get for the total. A developer at $95/hr who scopes correctly and ships in three weeks is cheaper than a developer at $55/hr who underscopes, hits blockers, and drags for six.

I've built over 40 Webflow projects across startups, SaaS companies, agencies, and solo founders. The pricing questions I get are almost always the same: Is this quote reasonable? Should I go cheaper? What am I actually paying for? This post answers all of them with real numbers, not marketing copy.

If you already know what you need and want to skip straight to a conversation, [get in touch](/contact).

## The short answer: Webflow developer cost by project type

Here's where projects actually land. These ranges assume a senior developer, a Figma design ready before build starts, and no extreme integrations or custom-coded components.

| Project type | Range |
|---|---|
| Landing page (1–3 pages, no CMS) | $1,500–$3,500 |
| 5-page marketing site | $3,000–$6,500 |
| 10-page marketing site (with CMS) | $4,500–$9,000 |
| CMS-heavy site (25+ pages, full blog, case studies) | $10,000–$22,000 |
| WordPress or Squarespace migration | $3,500–$9,000 |
| Monthly retainer (10–40 hrs/month) | $1,500–$5,000/mo |

These are not bottom-of-the-market numbers. They reflect what a senior specialist charges — someone who has built dozens of Webflow sites, thinks about CMS architecture before touching the Designer, knows which interactions tank Lighthouse scores, and doesn't need to Google how reference fields work. If you want a cheaper number, you'll find one. Whether it produces a cheaper outcome is a different question.

For a broader comparison of what type of person to hire for your project size, see the [freelancer vs agency breakdown](/blog/webflow-freelancer-vs-agency) — the decision there directly affects your total cost.

## Hourly rates by geography and seniority

The hourly market in 2026 is genuinely global, and geography matters more than people expect. Here's how rates break down across the tiers I actually encounter when projects come through referral or Upwork.

| Region | Junior | Mid | Senior |
|---|---|---|---|
| North America | $45–$65/hr | $70–$95/hr | $95–$140/hr |
| Western Europe | $40–$60/hr | $65–$90/hr | $90–$130/hr |
| Eastern Europe | $25–$40/hr | $40–$65/hr | $60–$95/hr |
| South Asia / Latin America | $18–$35/hr | $35–$60/hr | $55–$85/hr |

Under $45/hr for a North American developer is a junior or offshore generalist. Full stop. That's not a judgment — juniors can handle simple builds — but you need to know what you're buying. If your project has a complex CMS, custom animations, or CRM integrations, a junior developer will cost you more in revisions and fixes than the rate difference saves.

The mid-market sweet spot for most projects is $65–$95/hr for a North American or Western European senior developer, or $60–$85/hr for an Eastern European senior. Above $120/hr you're typically paying for agency overhead, a narrow niche (enterprise Webflow with custom APIs), or a specialist with a track record you can verify.

Hourly rates also correlate heavily with what a developer can produce in a day. A senior developer builds 2–3 well-crafted, responsive pages per day. A junior struggles with 1, and the QA round takes twice as long. The effective project cost — not the hourly cost — is what matters, and [how long a build takes](/blog/webflow-build-timeline) explains exactly how those days add up across different project types.

## Fixed-price projects: 5-page, 10-page, 25-page

Most Webflow engagements are fixed-price, not time-and-materials. That's usually better for the client — you know your budget — but only if the scope is locked before the number is agreed. Here's what each size actually includes and what drives the price within each range.

**5-page marketing site: $3,000–$6,500**

Five pages is the typical early-stage startup site: homepage, about, services or product, pricing, and contact. At $3,000 you're getting someone who builds fast with limited responsiveness checking. At $6,500 you're getting a developer who treats each page as a design system problem — consistent component structure, a CMS for anything that might need to change, pixel-perfect responsiveness at every breakpoint, and a Lighthouse score above 85 at launch.

The biggest cost variable at this size: whether the developer designs in Webflow from scratch or builds from a Figma file. Design adds $1,500–$3,000 depending on complexity.

**10-page marketing site: $4,500–$9,000**

This is the most common scope I build. Ten pages usually means homepage, about, services (listing + one or two detail pages), blog index, blog post template, and contact. The CMS is not optional at this size — you need it for blog posts and often for team members, case studies, or services.

At the lower end of this range ($4,500–$6,000), expect standard Webflow interactions, basic CMS structure, and one or two integrations (contact form routing, basic analytics). At the upper end ($7,500–$9,000), expect scroll-triggered animations, a multi-collection CMS with reference fields, more complex integrations (Mailchimp or HubSpot sync, Calendly embed, Loom embeds with lazy loading), and a performance audit at launch.

Add $2,000–$4,000 if you need a full case study system with rich content, a filterable project gallery, or any custom JavaScript logic that goes beyond what Webflow Interactions handle natively.

**25-page site / CMS-heavy: $10,000–$22,000**

At 25 pages you're almost always dealing with multiple CMS collections, several page templates, and some amount of cross-collection referencing. The CMS architecture at this scale is genuinely hard to get right. I've seen companies spend $15,000 on a 25-page site, then spend another $8,000 six months later restructuring the CMS because the original developer built it like a database novice.

Real work at this scale: designing the CMS schema first (usually 3–6 collections with reference fields), building a global design system before touching individual pages, and ensuring the Webflow Editor experience is usable for a non-technical content manager who will be adding pages after launch.

You can see the kinds of results that level of thinking produces in my [work portfolio](/work). The specifics vary by client, but the common thread is sites that work as content systems, not just collections of pretty pages.

## Webflow retainers: monthly bands and what they include

Retainers are where the pricing gets more nuanced, because what you're actually buying varies significantly across the range. If you're thinking about whether a retainer or a project engagement fits your situation better, the detailed breakdown is in [retainer vs project pricing](/blog/webflow-retainer-vs-project) — but here's the practical summary.

**$1,500/month (10–15 hours)**

A lean retainer for a mostly-stable site. What this covers: two to four content updates or small page tweaks per month, one minor new section or landing page per quarter, bug fixes and browser compatibility checks, and one SEO update cycle. What it doesn't cover: new page templates, design system changes, or anything that involves restructuring the CMS.

This tier suits a company that launched a site 6–12 months ago and mostly needs to keep it current. It does not suit a fast-moving marketing team pushing new campaign pages every month.

**$2,500–$3,500/month (20–30 hours)**

The most common retainer size I operate on. This covers: two to three new landing pages per month (built from the existing design system, not from scratch), ongoing CMS updates, one integration change or addition per month, A/B test support (typically I'm building the variant in Webflow, not running the test tooling), and monthly performance and Core Web Vitals checks.

At this level you have someone who knows your site well enough to move fast. New pages shouldn't need a full brief — a Figma frame and a content doc is enough. That context value is worth something, and it's a legitimate part of what you're paying for.

**$3,500–$5,000/month (30–40 hours)**

Active growth team retainer. New pages weekly or close to it, regular A/B testing, CMS expansion as new product lines or markets are added, integration work with the marketing stack (HubSpot, Salesforce, custom webhooks), and first-response bug fixes within 24 hours. At this level, the Webflow developer is effectively part of the marketing team.

If you're evaluating whether to hire a part-time contractor or a senior Webflow freelancer on retainer for this volume of work, the retainer usually wins on flexibility — you're not paying benefits or managing payroll, and you can scale up or down with a month's notice.

## What makes a Webflow project expensive (or cheap)

Price within any project category isn't random. These are the five variables that move quotes by 30–50% in either direction.

**Design status.** Building from a finished Figma file with defined components and a type system is 40–60% faster than designing in Webflow from scratch. If you hand a developer a Figma file that has one "desktop" frame with no mobile consideration and no component structure, that's barely faster than starting from scratch — and some developers price it like starting from scratch, because that's what it is in practice.

**CMS complexity.** A simple blog (one collection, standard fields) adds maybe four to six hours. A full content system with case studies, team members, services, and cross-referenced categories adds twenty to forty. I've written in detail about the decisions behind this in [how to hire a Webflow developer](/blog/hire-webflow-developer-2026), but the short version is: the schema decisions you make upfront determine how much everything costs to change later.

**Animations and interactions.** Standard fade-ins and slide-ups cost almost nothing in Webflow — it's a settings panel, not code. What costs money is complex scroll-triggered sequences (especially if they need to coordinate across multiple elements), Lottie animations (source file quality matters enormously), and any interaction that needs JavaScript because Webflow Interactions can't express it. Quote for animations should always specify what interactions are included. "Custom animations" is too vague to price honestly.

**Integration count and complexity.** Each integration has a baseline cost: Calendly embed is two hours, HubSpot form sync is four to six, Memberstack setup is eight to twelve, a custom Airtable sync is fifteen to twenty-five. Developers who quote without listing integrations explicitly are setting up a scope dispute.

**Content delivery timing.** This one won't show up in a quote, but it shows up in the invoice. Projects where content arrives late, in pieces, or in a different format than agreed always run longer. The clock doesn't stop while a developer waits for the hero copy to be approved by the CMO. If you don't have a clear content delivery plan before a project starts, budget a 20% schedule buffer at minimum.

## Where people overpay (and where they skimp and regret it)

After 40+ projects, I've seen both patterns enough to be specific about them.

<Callout type="warning">
The biggest way buyers overpay: hiring a full-service agency to build a simple 5–7 page site. Agency overhead — account managers, project managers, internal handoffs between design and development — adds 40–80% to the effective cost of work that a senior freelancer can deliver with less coordination overhead and more direct accountability. A 5-page site should not cost $18,000. If it does, you're paying for process, not outcomes.
</Callout>

**Other places people overpay:**

Paying a premium for "custom animations" that are Webflow's native Interactions panel with slightly more steps. I've quoted against competitors who charged $3,000 for a scroll-triggered stagger effect that takes me 45 minutes. Ask the developer to show you in Webflow Designer how they plan to build the animation. If it's IX2 settings, it should be priced accordingly.

Paying for SEO "setup" as an add-on. Basic SEO configuration — meta titles, descriptions, Open Graph tags, sitemap, robots.txt, canonical tags, structured data — should be part of every Webflow launch, not a $1,500 line item. The only legitimate SEO add-ons are keyword research, on-page copy optimization, or technical audits of an existing site. If a developer quotes "SEO setup" separately for a new build, push back.

Paying agency rates for project management on simple scopes. A 10-page site with a senior freelancer does not need a project manager. You get a weekly update and a shared task board. The PM layer is appropriate at $25K+ projects spanning multiple disciplines.

**Where people skimp and regret it:**

Skipping the design system phase. Developers who start building pages immediately — without defining a type scale, a spacing system, a component library, and a color set — produce sites that look coherent at 100% zoom and fall apart when you add a page the developer didn't build. Every page after launch becomes a manual effort.

Accepting vague handoff. "I'll walk you through it on a call" is not a handoff. You want: a documented class naming convention, a style guide page inside the Webflow project, written notes on every custom embed and third-party script, and a screen recording covering the Editor. Without this, every future change costs you a support conversation with the developer or a full-cost discovery with someone new.

Buying the cheapest quote on a migration. WordPress-to-Webflow migrations are where I see the most regret on low bids. The cheap quote usually means: manually rebuild in Webflow, no CMS-to-CMS structure mapping, no redirect strategy for SEO, no content cleanup. The result is a site that looks new but performs worse in search than the WordPress site did — which is genuinely hard to undo six months later.

## What a fair quote looks like

A quote you can trust has seven things. If any are missing, ask for them before signing.

**1. Page list by name.** Not "approximately 10 pages" — the actual list: Homepage, About, Services, Pricing, Blog Index, Blog Post, Contact. If the developer can't list the pages, they haven't scoped the project.

**2. CMS collections named.** Blog Posts, Case Studies, Team Members — whatever applies. Each collection should be listed with the approximate number of fields and whether it references another collection.

**3. Integrations listed with time estimates.** "HubSpot form sync: 5 hours" is useful. "Integrations as needed" is not a scope document.

**4. Animations described specifically.** "Scroll-triggered fade and stagger on homepage sections" is specific. "Modern animations" is a sales line.

**5. Deliverables at launch.** Style guide page, class naming document, Editor walkthrough recording, staging URL for review, redirect map if migrating from an existing site.

**6. Revision policy.** How many rounds? What counts as a revision vs. a scope change? A two-revision limit with a written change order process for scope additions is the standard I work to and the standard you should expect.

**7. Content delivery requirements.** What you need to provide and by when. A developer quoting a four-week timeline but leaving content delivery undefined is quoting a four-week timeline conditional on things you haven't committed to yet.

A quote that has all seven of those things is a quote from someone who has built enough projects to know what goes wrong. A quote that has two of them — a page count and a price — is a guess.

<Callout type="tip">
Before accepting any quote, send the developer a one-line reply: "Can you send a page-by-page list with CMS collections, integrations, and your revision policy?" If they can respond with that in 24 hours, they've actually scoped the project. If they push back or go vague, that's the answer.
</Callout>

The number that matters is not the lowest number. It's the number attached to a scope you can hold someone accountable to.
