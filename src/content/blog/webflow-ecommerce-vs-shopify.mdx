The answer to **webflow ecommerce vs shopify** is not "it depends" — it is a clear decision tree based on SKU count, revenue, and how much your brand story depends on the storefront itself. Under 100 SKUs, design-led, sub-$200K annual revenue: Webflow wins on flexibility and total cost. Over 500 SKUs, subscriptions, multi-warehouse fulfillment, or aggressive app integrations: Shopify wins by a wide margin. What sits in between is where the analysis actually matters, and that's what this post is for.

I covered [the full capabilities of Webflow Ecommerce in a separate post](/blog/webflow-ecommerce-2026) if you want the platform-only view. This post assumes you already know both platforms exist and want to know which one to build on. If you're already leaning toward Shopify and wondering how to keep Webflow's design quality, I'll cover the hybrid pattern in section 8. And if you want to skip the analysis and just talk through your specific store, [get in touch](/contact).

## Quick verdict

| Scenario | Pick Webflow | Pick Shopify |
|---|---|---|
| Under 100 SKUs, design-first brand | Yes | — |
| Over 500 SKUs, fast catalog growth | — | Yes |
| Subscription or recurring billing | — | Yes (Recharge) |
| Bespoke visual storytelling on PDPs | Yes | — |
| Multi-warehouse / 3PL fulfillment | — | Yes |
| Need dozens of third-party app integrations | — | Yes |
| Developer not available — self-manage content | — | Yes |

The table above is binary for readability, but the reality is that a 150-SKU apparel brand with a strong visual identity and no plans for subscriptions could go either way. That's the band where the rest of this post does its work. The right answer also changes over time — a store that starts on Webflow for its design advantages may outgrow it in 18 months if growth means operational complexity. Build for where you'll be in two years, not just where you are today.

## Design and theming freedom

This is where Webflow has no peer in the ecommerce space, including Shopify. Shopify's design system is theme-based: you pick a theme from the Theme Store (or build a custom Liquid theme from scratch), and every page inherits from that theme's section and block architecture. Getting truly custom layout behavior — a product page where the image gallery behaves differently at different scroll depths, or a collection page that shows editorial copy between product rows — requires Liquid template editing and JavaScript you write yourself.

Webflow's Designer gives you full pixel control over every element on every page: product pages, collection pages, cart, the checkout flow. You can build a product detail page that feels more like an editorial campaign than a product listing. Every interaction — hover states, image swaps, scroll-triggered reveals — is native in the Designer without touching code. For brands where the storefront is the brand experience, that's not a marginal advantage. It's the whole reason to be on the platform.

The tradeoff is that this power requires someone who knows how to use it. A Webflow Ecommerce build at the quality level that justifies choosing it over Shopify is not a self-service project. That's true of both platforms at a serious level, but the design ceiling Webflow enables is genuinely higher — which means it also requires more skill to reach.

One specific place this shows up: collection page layouts. On Shopify, a collection page is a grid of product cards derived from theme settings. Adding editorial rows, category headers with custom imagery, or scroll-triggered category filters requires significant theme development. On Webflow, those are native Designer operations — no Liquid, no JavaScript required. For brands that use collection pages as discovery surfaces rather than just category buckets, this is a material difference.

I covered this dynamic in the [Webflow vs Shopify overview post](/blog/webflow-vs-shopify) if you want the broader platform comparison beyond just ecommerce.

## Product catalog and variants

Webflow Ecommerce supports product variants through a field-based system in the CMS: you define properties (Size, Color, Material), add SKU combinations, and set individual pricing and inventory per SKU. For stores with a manageable catalog, this is fine. For stores where variant complexity explodes — 10 colors × 8 sizes × 3 materials = 240 SKUs per product, across 200 products — Webflow starts showing its limits. Bulk editing is limited. CSV imports work but require care. There is no native way to manage inventory across locations.

Shopify was built around product and variant management. Its admin UI is the gold standard for merchants: bulk editing, inventory transfers between locations, barcode scanning, purchase orders, and demand forecasting tools (on higher plans). Its variant system supports up to three options per product (same as Webflow in practice), but the surrounding operational tooling — what happens after the variant is created — is in a different league.

Webflow also caps collection items. The Business plan allows 10,000 CMS items, which sounds like enough until you realize that each SKU combination often generates a separate item depending on how your catalog is structured. For small catalogs with rich visual treatment per product, Webflow is excellent. For operational ecommerce at volume, the catalog management gap is real.

One practical implication: if you run seasonal collections and regularly archive products, you'll hit Webflow's item limits faster than you expect. Shopify has no comparable hard cap on products. It also supports draft products, product availability scheduling, and inventory forecasting across locations — features that sound secondary until your operation depends on them daily.

## Checkout and conversion

Shopify's checkout is the best-converting checkout in the industry at scale. That claim is not puffery — it's the result of being A/B tested across hundreds of thousands of live stores simultaneously, with Shopify pooling learnings across the entire network. Shop Pay is the sharpest edge: it autofills payment and shipping for any customer who has checked out on any Shopify store before, which is a massive percentage of online shoppers. Shop Pay adds a measurable 5–10% lift on mobile conversion in A/B tests across the Shopify merchant base.

Webflow's checkout is clean and customizable — you can match it to your brand without fighting a theme system — but it has fewer behavioral optimizations out of the box. No network-effect autofill, no accelerated checkout buttons for Apple Pay or PayPal at the same level of integration depth, no built-in A/B testing framework for checkout step variants.

<Callout type="tip">
If you're building on Webflow Ecommerce and want to close the conversion gap with Shopify, add Stripe's Payment Request Button for native Apple Pay / Google Pay support via a custom embed, configure address autocomplete through Google Places API, and reduce form fields to the minimum required. You cannot replicate Shop Pay's network autofill natively, but you can eliminate the friction that matters most for first-time buyers.
</Callout>

For stores where conversion rate is the primary KPI and mobile drives 60%+ of traffic, Shopify's checkout advantage is not something you can fully engineer around on Webflow. For stores where the average order value is high and the buyer journey is deliberate — luxury goods, made-to-order, artisan products — checkout friction matters less and brand experience matters more. Know which store you're building.

There's also a guest checkout and account management gap. Shopify's customer accounts are feature-rich by default: order history, address management, return initiation, loyalty program hooks. Webflow's customer accounts are functional but thinner, and adding meaningful post-purchase account features requires custom code or third-party tools. If repeat purchase rate and customer lifetime value are core to your model, Shopify's account infrastructure earns its keep.

## Apps and extensibility

Shopify's app marketplace is the single biggest reason mature ecommerce operations choose Shopify and stay there. With over 10,000 apps available, almost every operational problem has a tested, supported solution: Recharge for subscriptions, ShipStation or ShipBob for fulfillment, Klaviyo for email, Gorgias for customer support, Avalara for automated tax compliance, Yotpo for reviews, Afterpay or Affirm for buy-now-pay-later. These integrations have been built by dedicated teams, are actively maintained, and have customer bases of thousands of merchants providing ongoing feedback.

<Callout type="warning">
Webflow Ecommerce has no comparable app ecosystem. There is no native subscription billing solution, no multi-warehouse inventory management, no mature buy-now-pay-later integration, and no tax compliance automation tool at the level of Avalara. If your business model depends on any of these, you should not build on Webflow Ecommerce — the engineering cost of replicating what a $29/month Shopify app handles will exceed the cost of just using Shopify.
</Callout>

Webflow's Logic automation (its built-in workflow tool) and native Zapier/Make compatibility handle simpler integrations: form submissions to a CRM, order notifications to Slack, new customer data to Klaviyo. For a design-led brand that needs email marketing, review collection, and basic order management, that's often enough. Once you need subscriptions, 3PL integration, or anything approaching operational complexity, Webflow's extensibility ceiling becomes a hard constraint.

## Marketing and SEO tools

Webflow Ecommerce has a genuine edge over Shopify in technical SEO baseline quality. Webflow generates clean, semantic HTML with a simple DOM structure and no unnecessary wrapper elements. Page weight is lower by default. Lighthouse scores on a well-built Webflow storefront are typically higher than on an equivalent Shopify theme. Per-page meta control — title, description, OG tags, canonical, schema — is straightforward and doesn't require a separate SEO app.

Shopify's default themes ship with more code bloat than they need. Getting a Shopify storefront to 90+ on mobile Lighthouse typically requires theme surgery: removing unused polyfills, deferring third-party scripts, optimizing image handling. It's achievable, but it requires deliberate work. Adding SEO control beyond what the theme admin exposes often requires an app (SEO Booster, Smart SEO) or a Liquid-level edit.

For a detailed walkthrough of what a well-optimized Webflow build looks like at the SEO layer, the [Webflow SEO checklist for 2026](/blog/webflow-seo-checklist-2026) covers every technical touchpoint. The principles apply directly to Webflow Ecommerce product and collection pages.

On the marketing content side, Webflow's CMS lets you build editorial landing pages, campaign microsites, and blog content with the same design freedom as the rest of the site — important for brands that use content as a discovery channel. Shopify's blog and page builder have improved significantly in recent years, but you're still working within a theme's section-and-block constraints for anything beyond standard layouts. A Webflow store can have a genuinely great editorial blog, a lookbook section with full-bleed CMS-driven imagery, and rich category landing pages — all built and maintained through the CMS without touching code. That kind of content marketing infrastructure is meaningful for stores competing on organic search in a crowded product category. Shopify with a custom Liquid theme can get there too, but the development investment is higher and the content editor experience is less intuitive for non-technical teams.

## Pricing and TCO over 3 years

Platform fees alone don't tell the story. Total cost of ownership includes platform fees, transaction fees, app subscriptions, and developer time to build and maintain the store.

| Year | Webflow total | Shopify total |
|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | $3,800–$12,000 | $5,200–$18,000 |
| Year 2 | $2,200–$5,000 | $3,600–$14,000 |
| Year 3 | $1,800–$4,000 | $3,200–$12,000 |

Assumptions: Webflow Business plan ($39/mo), Shopify plan ($79/mo), 2–4 apps at $20–$80/mo each, and Year 1 including build costs (Webflow $3,000–$10,000, Shopify $4,000–$12,000). Shopify's app costs compound — each new operational requirement typically means another subscription. By Year 3, a Shopify store running 6–8 apps is paying $200–$500/month in app fees alone, on top of the base plan. Webflow's app gap means lower recurring costs but potential one-time engineering spend when you hit a feature ceiling.

The crossover point is roughly at $150K–$200K annual revenue with a catalog that is growing meaningfully and starting to need operational tooling. Below that, Webflow's lower ongoing cost and higher design quality win the TCO comparison for the right type of store. Above it, Shopify's ecosystem scales in ways Webflow can't replicate.

Transaction fees are a significant factor that often gets overlooked in initial comparisons. Webflow charges 2% on the Standard plan and 0% on Business and above. Shopify charges 0.5%–2% depending on plan (0% if using Shopify Payments, which requires a supported country). At $50K/month in revenue on Webflow Standard, that 2% fee is $1,000/month — upgrade to Business ($39/mo) and it's gone. Know which plan your revenue level actually requires.

## Who should pick which

**Pick Webflow Ecommerce when:** your catalog is comfortably under 200 SKUs with no plans to scale past that, your brand story depends on bespoke visual design on product and collection pages, you don't need subscriptions or 3PL fulfillment, and your SEO + content strategy is a primary acquisition channel. Luxury goods, artisan products, made-to-order items, design-first apparel brands, and physical goods sold alongside premium editorial content all sit squarely in Webflow's zone.

**Pick Shopify when:** your catalog is already large or growing fast, you need subscriptions, multi-warehouse inventory, automated tax compliance, or buy-now-pay-later at the checkout. Also pick Shopify when the person managing the store day-to-day is not technical — Shopify's admin is the industry standard for merchant self-service, and your team will be faster in it without training. If you're expecting rapid sales growth and want to reach for an ecosystem of solutions rather than engineer custom ones, Shopify's infrastructure handles it.

**The hybrid option** is worth naming explicitly for brands squarely caught in between: marketing site and campaign pages on Webflow (full design freedom, best SEO), product detail pages optionally on Webflow pulling from Shopify's API, and checkout always on Shopify. This keeps brand experience high on the pages that drive discovery while routing all transactional flow through Shopify's proven checkout and operations stack. I covered the exact architecture and tradeoffs in [the Shopify-to-Webflow hybrid post](/blog/shopify-to-webflow-hybrid) — worth reading if you're considering this path.

Under 100 SKUs and design-led: Webflow. Over 500 SKUs, subscriptions, or multi-warehouse: Shopify. In between, do the math on app costs, transaction fees, and build cost against the actual value of design freedom for your specific store.
